How to concentrate, according to the Buddha

Are you really present, or only riding a mental high?

1690737000

I've been having trouble concentrating this week, so I decided to revisit the suttas. The concept of metacognition, or thinking about thinking, stood out to me while scanning the section on concentration in the Dutiyavibhaṅgasutta (SN 48.10). The text tells us that finding joy in deep concentration is a step towards what Bhikkhu Sujato translates as full "immersion". Yet as long as we're analyzing how we focus, we're not really present.

I think Bhikkhu Sujato's choice of the word "immersion" is fitting. We can't just dip a toe in concentration — we need to fully dive in. As we submerge ourselves in the waters of the present moment, there is no room for the hot air of our egos and opinions. There is no more pleasure or pain swirling around us, just cool, deep equanimity.

I found this idea so powerful because it made me recognize all the times I stopped short of a real "flow state". Even when I'm the most productive, I'm either filled with pride or desperately trying to get the job over with. The ego is always there, standing in the way of pure mindfulness.

This also connects to the idea of practicing just to practice. I often meditate to feel better, but that shouldn't be the goal. Chasing after peace and running from discomfort is all clinging. If we're doing something just to feel good or get it over with, we're not present.

I read a line by Thich Nhat Hanh yesterday that said something like, "washing this dish is the most important job in the world." Every task should be approached that way. Surgeons and bomb squads aren't concentrating if they're thinking about how they're thinking.

Their only job is the present moment. Make it yours also.

Saṁyuttanikāya — Linked Discourses Collection — (SN)

The “Linked” or “Connected” Discourses (Saṁyutta Nikāya, abbreviated SN) is a collection of over a thousand short discourses in the Pali canon. The word “linked” refers to the fact that the texts are collected and organized by topic. In most cases the organizing principle is a particular theme of Dhamma, for example, the five aggregates, dependent origination, the noble eightfold path, mindfulness meditation, or the four noble truths. This collection contains the most extensive range of texts on these core themes. In other cases chapters are organized according to the person or kind of person who speaks. This collection has a full parallel in the Saṁyuktāgama (SA) of the Sarvāstivāda school in Chinese translation. In addition, there are two partial collections in Chinese (SA-2 and SA-3) as well as a number of miscellaneous or fragmentary texts in Chinese, Sanskrit, and Tibetan. Much of the organizational structure of SN is shared with SA, suggesting that this structure preceded the split between these two collections.

Mahāvaggasaṁyutta — The Group of Linked Discourses on the Path

The “Great Book” is the last and largest of the five books of the Linked Discourses. It consists of twelve saṁyuttas, almost all of which deal with an aspect of Buddhist practice, or the path. The first of these, indeed, is the “Section on the Path” (Maggasaṁyutta), and in the northern canons the book as a whole is referred to as the “Book of the Path” (Maggavagga). The first seven of the saṁyuttas treat the familiar set of seven groups of factors on Buddhist practice known to the later traditions as the bodhipakkhiyā dhammā, or “qualities leading to awakening”. This book is the primary source for these teachings. Subsequent saṁyuttas deal with the path from different perspectives, while the final two deal with stream-entry and the four noble truths respectively.

Indriyasaṁyutta — Linked Discourses on the Faculties — (SN 48)

The “Linked Discourses on the Faculties” contains 178 discourses on various sets of “faculties” (indriya). The “five faculties” of faith, energy, mindfulness, immersion, and wisdom are the most natural fit in this context. However the chapter also finds room for discourses on a wider range of 22 faculties, including the six sense faculties, five affective faculties (kinds of feeling), three faculties relating the process of enlightenment, and three concerning biology and gender. This flexible category thus serves as a link between the teachings on wisdom and those on the path.

Suddhikavagga — The Chapter on the Plain Version — (SN 48.1–10)

Dutiyavibhaṅgasutta — (SN 48.10)

The Buddha gives a detailed explanation of each of the five faculties.

sn48.10:0.1 Linked Discourses 48.10
sn48.10:0.2 1. Plain Version
sn48.10:0.3 Analysis (2nd)

sn48.10:1.1 “Mendicants, there are these five faculties.
sn48.10:1.2 What five?
sn48.10:1.3 The faculties of faith, energy, mindfulness, immersion, and wisdom.
sn48.10:1.4 And what is the faculty of faith?
sn48.10:1.5 It’s when a noble disciple has faith in the Realized One’s awakening:
sn48.10:1.6 ‘That Blessed One is perfected, a fully awakened Buddha, accomplished in knowledge and conduct, holy, knower of the world, supreme guide for those who wish to train, teacher of gods and humans, awakened, blessed.’
sn48.10:1.7 This is called the faculty of faith.

sn48.10:2.1 And what is the faculty of energy?
sn48.10:2.2 It’s when a noble disciple lives with energy roused up for giving up unskillful qualities and embracing skillful qualities. They’re strong, staunchly vigorous, not slacking off when it comes to developing skillful qualities.
sn48.10:2.3 They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that bad, unskillful qualities don’t arise.
sn48.10:2.4 They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that bad, unskillful qualities that have arisen are given up.
sn48.10:2.5 They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that skillful qualities arise.
sn48.10:2.6 They generate enthusiasm, try, make an effort, exert the mind, and strive so that skillful qualities that have arisen remain, are not lost, but increase, mature, and are completed by development.
sn48.10:2.7 This is called the faculty of energy.

sn48.10:3.1 And what is the faculty of mindfulness?
sn48.10:3.2 It’s when a noble disciple is mindful. They have utmost mindfulness and alertness, and can remember and recall what was said and done long ago.
sn48.10:3.3 They meditate observing an aspect of the body—keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world.
sn48.10:3.4 They meditate observing an aspect of feelings …
sn48.10:3.5 mind …
sn48.10:3.6 principles—keen, aware, and mindful, rid of covetousness and displeasure for the world.
sn48.10:3.7 This is called the faculty of mindfulness.

sn48.10:4.1 And what is the faculty of immersion?
sn48.10:4.2 It’s when a noble disciple, relying on letting go, gains immersion, gains unification of mind.
sn48.10:4.3 Quite secluded from sensual pleasures, secluded from unskillful qualities, they enter and remain in the first absorption, which has the rapture and bliss born of seclusion, while placing the mind and keeping it connected.
sn48.10:4.4 As the placing of the mind and keeping it connected are stilled, they enter and remain in the second absorption, which has the rapture and bliss born of immersion, with internal clarity and mind at one, without placing the mind and keeping it connected.
sn48.10:4.5 And with the fading away of rapture, they enter and remain in the third absorption, where they meditate with equanimity, mindful and aware, personally experiencing the bliss of which the noble ones declare, ‘Equanimous and mindful, one meditates in bliss.’
sn48.10:4.6 Giving up pleasure and pain, and ending former happiness and sadness, they enter and remain in the fourth absorption, without pleasure or pain, with pure equanimity and mindfulness.
sn48.10:4.7 This is called the faculty of immersion.

sn48.10:5.1 And what is the faculty of wisdom?
sn48.10:5.2 It’s when a noble disciple is wise. They have the wisdom of arising and passing away which is noble, penetrative, and leads to the complete ending of suffering.
sn48.10:5.3 They truly understand: ‘This is suffering’ … ‘This is the origin of suffering’ … ‘This is the cessation of suffering’ … ‘This is the practice that leads to the cessation of suffering’.
sn48.10:5.4 This is called the faculty of wisdom.
sn48.10:5.5 These are the five faculties.”
